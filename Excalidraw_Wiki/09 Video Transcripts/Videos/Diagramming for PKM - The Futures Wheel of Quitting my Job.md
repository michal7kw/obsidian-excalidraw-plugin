# Summary
This video introduces the Futures Wheel, a method for exploring the consequences of decisions, using "quitting my job" as a central scenario. The presenter, Zsolt from Visual PKM, demonstrates how to construct a Futures Wheel by mapping primary, secondary, and tertiary impacts of quitting, categorizing them as risks or opportunities, and even leveraging GPT-4 for validation and gap analysis. He highlights both the negative and positive potential outcomes, emphasizing the tool's value for identifying both threats and opportunities associated with significant life changes and for informing strategic planning, further demonstrating how to link the diagram to Obsidian for expanded note-taking and idea generation.

# Key Takeaways
- The Futures Wheel is a simple yet powerful diagram for systematically exploring the primary, secondary, and tertiary consequences of a decision or event, aiding in future planning and risk assessment.
    
- Visualizing potential impacts across different time horizons (short-term, mid-term, long-term) helps to identify both positive opportunities and negative risks associated with a scenario, like quitting a job, allowing for proactive mitigation and strategic preparation.
    
- Tools like GPT-4 can be integrated to enhance the Futures Wheel process by providing unbiased feedback, challenging assumptions, and identifying potential gaps or logical errors in the analysis, leading to a more robust and well-rounded understanding of future scenarios.

In the next part, we'll develop a Sun Ray Transformation Map, turning our observations into an actionable roadmap. **The Futures Wheel**
The Futures Wheel is a simple method for thinking through the primary, secondary, and tertiary consequences of trends, events, emerging issues, and future decisions. It was invented in 1971 by Jerome C. Glenn, then a student at the Antioch University of New England. Since then, the Futures Wheel has been picked up by futurists, corporate planners, policy makers, and advisors worldwide to identify potential problems and opportunities and to assess tactics and strategies. [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=46)

**Adding the Central Scenario**
Let's start by adding our focus scenario, "Quitting my job," in the center. I will then draw three concentric ellipses representing the primary, secondary, and tertiary impacts or consequences of this scenario. The further out the ellipses, the more speculative our statements will be. [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=85)

**Primary Impacts**
Think with me. What would happen two or three months after you've actually quit? What are the most likely primary impacts or consequences of your decision? It's lucky that I have a time machine in my living room. Let's hop in and travel into the future to find out... Right after we quit, we'd immediately "lose our steady income" along with any "employee benefits" we may enjoy today. This will inevitably lead to some level of anxiety as the "uncertainty of the next paycheck" kicks in and as we start to "tap into our savings." But there's also an upside, the reason we wanted to quit in the first place! We'd have "more free time" to do what we really want with our time. Plus, even if the company and boss you work for isn't per se hostile, and is not forcing you to spend your weekends and evenings working, you will likely feel that office work is toxic, simply because you are spending the prime of your life working on things that aren't a hundred percent aligned with your passion. Hopefully, you're working on something that you find interesting, but even then, chances are that there are tasks and projects that do not fill you with energy; you just do them because they come with the job. Thus, you will likely experience an "emotional relief from a toxic work environment." However, quitting will also likely lead to a—let's hope, temporary—"identity crisis" since the job you've been doing for the past ten, twenty, maybe more years is suddenly gone. Additionally, not having a steady job, or giving up a prestigious career, can lead to "loss of status" and a "change in social perception." Finally, while you might have disliked your employer, you will surely miss at least some of your co-workers, the social atmosphere of the office. [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=128)

**Secondary Impacts**
At this point, you might think I am not optimistic enough... I won't debate that, but I believe this is the sober reality. Apart from the free time and emotional relief, there isn't much going for quitting, at least not in the short term. But let's turn the clock arm in our time machine to half a year after you've quit your job. Let's forget the original scenario at the very center of our diagram and instead think about impacts and consequences we collected on the first ring. What could be some of the consequences or impacts of each of the primary impacts? Well, the "loss of a steady income" will likely lead to "decreased financial stability" and will also require some "financial adjustments" such as eating out less frequently, canceling some subscriptions, and maybe not going on expensive holidays. Loss of the steady income and the loss of employee benefits will mean we had to "tap into our savings," which may also impact our "retirement planning." Notice how a particular secondary impact may be influenced by multiple primary ones. But of course, this is just one side of the coin. You'll surely engage in a "search for some additional income." With all this free time on your hands, you will think about "starting a business, or some other form of self-employment," which in turn will likely require you to invest more of your time into "self-development and reflection." After all, if you start a business, you'll likely need new skills, and you'll need all that reflection to ensure you do not pick a next career that does not fully align with your passions. Once you are done mapping out the secondary impacts, you can move on to the tertiary impacts. [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=270)

**Tertiary Impacts**
Hopping back into our time machine, let's travel five or ten years into the future. Continuing on the financial impacts, "decreased financial stability" and "financial adjustments" may likely have a knock-on impact on your dependents, such as your children, your spouse, or your parents. Financial instability could also "strain your relationships." "Self-employment" will result in a "shift in your career path" and hopefully result in a "new professional identity." Self-development will help you nurture "new talents and interests." In the positive scenario, your change will "improve your long-term health" outlook and lead to a "more fulfilling life." Spending more time with friends and family will "improve your relationships," and all of this will add up to a "change in lifestyle." [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=380)

**Risks vs. Opportunities**
But let's take a step back and look at our diagram. As you can see, not everything looks rosy. One way to differentiate threats from opportunities is to color them. I will quickly change all the threats and negative outcomes to red. Alternatively, you could create two wheels for two scenarios; one would be the happy scenario, the other your doomsday scenario. Looking at these two wheels side by side can be very instructional in identifying gaps and mitigations. [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=444)

**Validation with GPT4**
While the Futures Wheel is a simple and engaging tool for plotting future scenarios, there is also a risk. We don't have a crystal ball. The wheel is no better than the collective judgements of those involved in creating it. One way you could counterbalance this inherent weakness of the tool is to use Chat GPT 4 to read and critique your work. I can simply copy the image of my Futures Wheel to the clipboard by exporting the selected elements as a PNG to the clipboard and pasting it to GPT4 and requesting GPT 4 to challenge the diagram and highlight gaps or logical errors in my thinking. This way, Chat GPT can function as an unbiased third party providing feedback on your work and ideas. [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=482)

**Adding Details with Wiki Links**
Finally, before we close, I wanted to show you how you can start to branch from this map by converting some, or all, of the impacts to their own Obsidian notes. By simply modifying the text "Starting a business" into a wiki link, the text element becomes a link. If I now navigate to this link, I can create an empty page or drawing, one that is linked to my Futures Wheel, and start brainstorming business ideas. [* ](https://youtu.be/Y5u7ggrxM1k?t=545)
