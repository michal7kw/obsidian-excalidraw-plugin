# Summary
In this video, Zsolt shares his progress on self-publishing his book, emphasizing the concept of "Progressive Elaboration" as a writing strategy. He discusses his writing process, the challenges of using AI tools to assist in crafting his book, and his excitement about the project as it develops. Zsolt reflects on the balance between speed and quality in planning for a successful launch by the March deadline.

# Key Takeaways
- Zsolt is integrating AI tools like ChatGPT and Google’s AI Studio into his writing process to create a cohesive book.
- He emphasizes the importance of "Progressive Elaboration" for refining ideas and improving the flow of content.
- Zsolt is dedicated to ensuring the quality of his book, even if it means taking extra time to make it right before self-publishing.

Hey everyone, Zsolt here! Welcome to what is it episode five, right? Episode five of my vlog about self-publishing a book. I'm on day 15 of my journey, and I really wish I was already here at the top. I'm still planning to present my journey, whether it's a failure or a success, at the PKM Summit in March. I hope you're going to like it! But back to our story. So I'm feeling like I'm right here. I've done some climbing, so we are now on the way up the top, but there are some extremely steep rocks that I still need to climb. I don't want to do it fast. I still want to do it fast, and we try, but I'm more interested in doing it right. [* ](https://youtu.be/RvwJSssLB8U?t=0)

Today, there's a single topic I want to talk to you about, and I call it Progressive Elaboration. But before we go there, I just wanted to show you that I've been playing with some cover art. Some people commented that there's too much going on in these. I like the colors, especially here. I like that it's vibrant, that it has color. I also like the title, "Visual Thinking for a Playful Creative Second Brain." For me, this subtitle speaks to the heart of the book. I would be interested in your view as well: which one do you like? I mean, I can still stick to my earlier design; this looks less dynamic, less engaging—maybe more focused. So this is also, I think, a reasonable design, but to me, this one here is just so much more engaging. But in terms of today's topic, this is my big learning from working on my book. I spent about 20 hours over the weekend; I spent most of Saturday and then from early morning till late night—something like from 6:00 in the morning until 10:00 PM on Sunday. I was sitting and writing, and this is my learning. So let me just walk you through what you can see here because I think there are some big lessons for me in terms of how you can work with GPT. At least today, as you know, I'm writing the book with the help of ChatGPT, Notebook LM, and Google's AI Studio with Gemini. [* ](https://youtu.be/RvwJSssLB8U?t=126)

Here's what I went through. Of course, in the beginning, I created this overview. I have a mind of the reader before reading the book and a reader after reading the book. I actually defined and wrote down who the readers are, and I crafted two paragraphs—one about the state of mind the reader is in at the beginning of the book and the state of mind the reader is in at the end. Actually, I wrote three pairs of paragraphs because I have three different types of readers in mind, and I think in episode two I talked about that, so you might want to check that out. Then I went ahead and created an outline, and don't look at the colored boxes yet; I'm going to explain to you what those are in a moment. Once I had this overall transformation, I created an outline that included the intro, the parts, and the epilog. Each of the parts had chapters, and indeed I broke down the chapters into this massive bullet point list into sections. In the end, I think in the previous episode—I mean in episode four—I showed you my outline. My outline ended up to be roughly 10,000 words. That's quite an elaborate outline, but it was broken down to the section level, and I had bullet points under each section. I thought I was done. I mean, I didn't think I was completely done, but I thought that I was ready to feed the beast. I was ready to feed ChatGPT with my own YouTube transcripts, with some of the reference material, and my outline, and out comes the book. This was all great, and it sort of worked like that, but not quite. The issue I ran into was that individually, each piece was good. I mean, sometimes GPT hallucinated; sometimes it completely misunderstood me. Then I had to go in and write down what I thought should actually be written. I found this process super engaging because I got upset: "Why did GPT not understand me?" Being upset made me super fast at typing out what I thought should be written down and then gave it to GPT, and it formatted it into very engaging and passionate sections. So I think as a reader, you're probably going to enjoy those sections the most. But the big issue was that these sections didn't fit. [* ](https://youtu.be/RvwJSssLB8U?t=264)

That's maybe another point to mention: what I found was that if I gave GPT an entire chapter—maybe a chapter of 3,000 to 4,000 words in length—I couldn't convince ChatGPT to produce that amount of cohesive text. I found that around 8,000 words, the performance starts to drop down, and really later on, you can open the text in the editor, and with GPT, you can work on it and increase it to maybe 1,500 words, but not more. That's where it breaks down. So I had to feed each chapter section by section to get a larger chapter produced, but then the issue was that these chapters weren't cohesive. They were individually crafted chapters that read nicely individually, but they did not have cohesion. So in the end, and this to me is now what seems to be a successful and workable approach: I went back to very beginning. Based on my transformation journey and of course on the detailed outline, I worked with GPT to create a transition script that was written in such a way that for each of the intro, part one, part two, part three, part four, and the epilogue, I asked GPT to write three different statements: the opening paragraph, the closing paragraph, and the transformation within that part. For each of these parts, I now have a couple of paragraphs of text. It starts with "This is how the part starts," "This is the objective and the outline content of that part," and "This is how the part ends." Then I took each of these and exploded them into a similar description for each chapter. So I asked GPT to take the detailed outline, take my descriptions of the transformation and the descriptions of the text for each of the parts, and then work it into a description for each of the chapters. Again, for each chapter, I asked GPT to outline the first paragraph, the last paragraph, and outline the contents within. Then, I did the same at a section-by-section level, and then I took each section and asked GPT to elaborate the text. What I found was that this way, GPT was able to generate enough context for itself, saved in these opening and closing paragraphs and the description of those sections, that now the text generated fits much nicer together. It's still not 100% fitting, and the steep mountain I showed in the beginning—it’s much like that rock climbing up here. I'm afraid this rock climbing is going to be when I pick up the entire draft and start reading it. Then I'm going to find these rough edges, and I think it’s going to be lots of manual work to clean up those rough edges. But yeah, this is where I am in my process. I call this Progressive Elaboration; it's sort of like Progressive Summarization, but the other way around. I'm in the midst of writing. I'm actually done with the intro, parts one, two, and three. I still have part four and the epilogue left to produce. I hope to do it in the next one to two days. I'm also planning to take two days off, so on Thursday and Friday, I'm not going to be working in the office. I'm going to stay home. Then I have four days, and I hope that by the end of the four days, I will have completed, of course, part four and the epilogue. Then I'll probably spend Thursday or maybe Friday reading the entire book and just making notes for myself. Then on Saturday, I can transfer those notes into the text using GPT, and then on Sunday, I can do a second draft read. Hopefully, I will have a reasonable draft. If I have that, then I think I'm still in time for self-publishing by the 15th of March. Though I don't want to rush it too much, because then I'm undermining my own sales. I want to have a proper launch for this book because I think this book is going to be good, and it deserves a proper launch. So, a bit of a long-winded vlog, but I don't have time for a shorter one. I also thought that this Progressive Elaboration is something that might interest you, so I wanted to talk a bit more in detail about this. Thank you! [* ](https://youtu.be/RvwJSssLB8U?t=765)
