# Summary
In this video, Zsolt introduces his unconventional visual newsletter, discussing its unique approach to thinking and communication through visuals. He emphasizes that thinking is not just a mental process but involves articulating and externalizing ideas, a concept supported by influential psychologists. The video encourages viewers to engage with visual thinking as a core tool for improving their cognitive processes and outlines practical methods for integrating visuals into their thinking.

# Key Takeaways
- Visual thinking is not merely an artistic skill; it is an essential cognitive tool that enhances clarity and reflection.
- Communication and thinking are intertwined, and articulating thoughts through various means (like drawing) is instrumental in idea formation.
- Practical tips for better visual thinking include using whiteboards for spatial organization, diagramming to make connections clearer, and iterating ideas visually.

Hey everyone, it's Jolt here. I started a newsletter, and I wanted to give you a quick feel for how it looks. It's an unconventional newsletter. It's visual, it's spatial, it's shared via Excalibur presentations, and if you want to sign up, you'll find the links in the video description. So, let's dive in. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=0)

In this second edition of my newsletter, I start out by talking about joining Dog Neil's core curriculum workshop. In his opening video for his course, he makes this statement: visual thinking isn't so much about what's happening up here, but it's about what's happening down here, pointing at the paper. So, it's less about thinking and more about making. This idea from Dog got me thinking because I believe he shifts the spotlight from our thoughts floating in our minds to marks taking shape. This simple move reframes thinking not as something shapeless we carry around but as something we make. It left me wondering with one of those deceptively simple questions that a three-year-old might ask you: where does thinking happen? At what point is an idea formed? And maybe more importantly, how does it happen? [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=25)

As I started to think about this, I realized that for centuries, people believed thinking happened in the heart. Today, I think if you were to ask the average person, they would say that thinking, of course, happens in the brain. But what if both of these notions are too narrow? What if we are asking the wrong question? I think that thinking and communication are the two sides of a coin. The more I contemplate this idea, the less difference I can identify between communication and thinking. I believe that a thought is not formed until it's shared. I'm not the only one who thinks so; for example, I found this quote from psychologist Lev Vygotsky: "Thought is not merely expressed in words; it comes into existence through them." These are links that you can click, and they will take you to the relevant source. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=56)

This means that ideas are not born fully formed; they are created when we articulate them. This articulation can be drawing, gestures, writing, and even art—like music or dance. There is also a close link between movement and thinking. David Kirsh, a cognitive scientist, argues that thinking evolved from our need to simulate movement. We needed this capability to plan our actions and understand what our actions would look like. This is the whole basis or the root of why we're thinking. When you're diagramming an idea, you're not just illustrating your thinking. Diagramming and drawing are actually doing the thinking—it is thought in motion. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=135)

If this is true, then visual thinking isn't just nice to have; it is a core tool for better thinking. And of course, if you want to watch the presentation by David Kirsh, here's the video link you can click. Why does visual thinking matter, especially now? I mean now, when we have such noise on Twitter, YouTube, and all sorts of social media, there's fake news and huge noise, which means we need to be able to think better. I imagine that your best thinking is not one thread but a strong braid. The first part is text; this is linear and word-heavy, which is what we've learned in school. Then, there's the embodied and spatial aspect; this is the making of ideas, thinking through action, and arranging information. This is what Dog highlights in his intro video. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=254)

Then there's the visual aspect, leveraging the fact that close to one-third of our brain's cortex is dedicated to visual processing. A study by Fessen from 2003 shows that roughly 27% of the cortex is focused on visual processing, which is the biggest portion of our brain. This means we are wired for this rich, braided thinking, but we're still often stuck with text. I'm not advocating that you should abandon text; I'm advocating that you should weave in visuals and spatial and embodied methods to communicate and think more clearly and remember what truly matters. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=327)

I want to highlight that visual thinking is not about being an artist or being able to draw. It's about externalizing your ideas so you can see them in context and reflect on them more effectively. So let's look at these four ideas here. The first one I titled "step out of line." Instead of writing a linear document and creating walls of text, I advocate for using a whiteboard to make your thinking spatial, not just sequential. Sketch before you write. I talk about this in a video about concept modeling. If you map out your ideas first, then your ideas will be clearer, and your argument will be more persuasive. If you're trying to fix a broken workflow or make a decision, you can diagram it. That will help you see connections and will lead to much better decisions. Finally, iterate visually. For example, in "dump, lump, jump," I discuss using sticky notes and a digital canvas to move ideas around and to first brainstorm, then organize, and finally form action plans. These are four simple ways you can start to think better using visuals today. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=379)

Finally, let me get back to this point about thinking as communication. Thinking doesn't live just in your head; it lives in the space between you and the page, between you and other people. When you make your ideas visible, you're not just clarifying them for others; you're clarifying them for yourself. Thinking visually is faster, more fun, and far more effective than just looping your ideas in your mind. Here's a quote from Wittgenstein stating that "the limits of my language are the limits of my mind." I would argue that maybe this is why drawing ideas expands the mind so powerfully because you're adding a new language, a visual language to think thoughts that are impossible through words alone. You're introducing additional language which expands the limits of your mind. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=454)

Pushing the envelope, for example, when a fighter jet pilot pushes his airplane beyond the specified limitations and achieves outstanding performance. Our basic go-to is to write ideas down to externalize your inner dialogue, and that’s already a huge step. You can take this further by drawing out your ideas, unlocking thoughts through this new language. Then you can also act out your ideas; you can embody your ideas. Finally, you can share it. You can invite fresh perspectives to complete the cycle. I can't state enough how much sharing on YouTube, sharing on my blog, and sharing in my course has changed my thinking. It has expanded my ideas far beyond when I wasn't sharing, keeping everything to myself. I believe that sharing is a core component of better thinking for yourself. [* ](https://youtu.be/CYW5v_yJSYg?t=550)

There's a survey, and I'm sharing the results of last week's survey, but we won't go into that too much right now.
