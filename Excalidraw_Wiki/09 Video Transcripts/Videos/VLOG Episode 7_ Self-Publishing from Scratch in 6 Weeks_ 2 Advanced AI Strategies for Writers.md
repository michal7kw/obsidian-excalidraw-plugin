# Content
Hey everyone, it's Jolt here. Five weeks ago, I announced a crazy experiment to speed write a book from scratch in six weeks. I'm not a writer, I'm not good with words, but I wanted to see if my visual thinking toolkit together with AI can bridge the gap and if I'm able to speed write a meaningful book. I also committed to maintaining the Vlog, and I recognize that for about two weeks I've been quiet. I've been neck-deep in work, working on the book as well as I committed to present my learnings, whether my failure or success or somewhere in between, at the Pam Summit in Utrecht, Netherlands, and that's just less than two weeks away. That's amazing—it's so close, and I'm so stressed about it! But I wanted to give you an update today. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=0)

In previous videos, I likened writing this book to climbing a mountain. I like these videos, and I also like to hike. This is speed climbing the Moern Horn, which is a 4,500-meter-high mountain in Switzerland. Of course, to speed climb a mountain like the Moern Horn, you need good climbing experience and knowledge of the area you're climbing. And you know what? I don't have that. But here are a couple of things that I'm going to share today. So first of all, I wanted to share some of my learnings about AI, and especially I wanted to show you two prompting strategies that I found that work for me. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=15)

What I found is that writing with AI is definitely not the easy way out. AI only amplifies what you already understand. If you don't have a clear idea of what you want to write about, AI can generate nice words, but it feels like Charlie Gordon after the surgery for moments. Then AI forgets and loses context, which means you need to re-explain and re-explain and re-correct. It's super frustrating. Sometimes it feels like dealing with someone who has Alzheimer's and has forgotten what you started discussing. So it's a frustrating experience to write with AI. But I have two successful strategies here. I wrote draft one, and I was super happy to finish it in three weeks; however, the celebration was short-lived because I saw that the material was plagued with repetition. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=30)

That's when I took a different approach and reverse-engineered Atomic notes from my initial draft. I thought my initial draft was good; it had strong ideas, but I needed to tease out the ideas from it. It’s sort of a reverse engineering of atomic notes. To illustrate my process, I referenced the movie "Prometheus," where an alien arrives on Earth, takes medicine, and dissolves into DNA, seeding new life. This parallels what Zettelkasten and atomic notes promise: that you have these atomic ideas you capture, process, and create something new. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=59)

So, what did I do? I used Google's AI Studio, Gemini, because version 2.0, which is available for testing, is free and can handle large context windows—up to 2 million tokens. I took all my material, created a prompt for AI Studio, telling it that it's a sensitive AI Insight extractor, and needed to find all the unique insights. I provided this long prompt with my first draft, and it generated a JSON file, which I processed into chapters. The file organized insights and references per chapter, resulting in a pretty comprehensive list of atomic ideas extracted from my book. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=113)

Next, I created a ChatGPT project folder, where I placed four items: writing instructions, the atomic ideas JSON, a detailed chapter outline for the entire book, and the draft I'd already written. After finishing the first draft, I thought about the book again and created a more robust outline that strictly defined what was out of scope for each chapter, ensuring no repetition. I have some strong opinions about certain topics that cluttered the book, so I made it clear every topic must have its chapter. As I progressed, I provided the draft that was already written to guide the AI. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=177)

Before writing each chapter, I asked GPT to list the key concepts for that chapter based on the insights and reference JSON and my outline. Sometimes, I received excellent lists of concepts, but other times, it was off the mark. This was crucial to ensure my context retained the right understanding and minimized pollution between chapters. I would adjust the outline based on the core concepts I received, leading me to the next step, where I would specify which chapter I wanted to write. I instructed GPT to ask me questions before it started writing, clarifying any ambiguities in the chapter outline. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=431)

I discovered I needed to break down chapters into smaller parts because a single document can handle longer token outputs, but maintaining quality decreases significantly after around 1,000 words. I would request the AI to split the chapter into two parts. While earlier models struggled, I found that the new GPT-4 could generate longer pieces, but my usage was limited, so I often paired it with GitHub's Copilot tools to refine language. After drafting part one of a chapter and ensuring it was satisfactory, I would then prompt GPT to write part two, ensuring I incorporated context from part one to enhance coherence. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=564)

This approach effectively allowed me to generate coherent chapters. Once my draft for part two was ready, I would integrate part one and part two and then request the AI to review the entire chapter against the outline and ensure there was no repetition or misalignment with the defined concepts. After this process, I found that around the second or third iteration, the output started going in circles, indicating the optimal number of revisions. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=629)

Ultimately, I completed draft two of the book and sent it to a few people for review. I feel much more comfortable with this draft. Will I publish this book by the end of the week? I doubt it, but I am primarily focused on quality. This is turning out to be a very good book, and if it takes two to three weeks longer, I'm fine with that—there's no external pressure. I'll be sharing my learnings at the PKM Summit, so if you haven't signed up yet and you're in Europe, check the link in the video description for a discount. I hope it still works! I'll share more about my learnings and insights on how my visual thinking toolkit has guided the writing process at the summit. [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=900)

Thank you for watching! I hope you found these two prompting strategies helpful. In the comments below, let me know what you think! You've already shared many valuable insights, tips, tricks, and tools—please keep them coming. I read all of them and apply them. I'm genuinely open to your feedback; let me know if you have a good idea. I hope to see you at the summit, where we can chat about the book, talk about PKM, visuals, and have a great time together. Thank you! [* ](https://youtu.be/sGzWvI7-Le0?t=1015)
